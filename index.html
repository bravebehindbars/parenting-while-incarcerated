Parents in Prison

In 2025, nearly half of all parents in state and federal prisons have children under 18.

Specifically, about 47% of state prisoners and 58% of federal prisoners are parents, according to the
Bureau of Justice Statistics Website.
This translates to a significant number of children affected by parental incarceration, with some estimates suggesting over 200,000 children each year have a parent in prison.

Below is a more detailed breakdown.

Impact On Children

The incarceration of a parent has far-reaching consequences for children, including increased risk of emotional, behavioral, and learning difficulties, according to the Institute for Family Studies. They may experience academic struggles, like lower grades, and be more likely to face disciplinary issues at school.

Behavioral Problems: These emotional challenges can manifest as behavioral problems, such as aggression, difficulty forming relationships, and social withdrawal.
Social Challenges: Relationships with peers and social interactions can be negatively affected, potentially leading to isolation and loneliness.
Increased Risk of Future Involvement in the Justice System: Studies show that children of incarcerated parents are at a higher risk of becoming involved in the juvenile or criminal justice system themselves.
Reduced Family Stability: The absence of a parent can disrupt family dynamics and create instability, impacting the child's sense of security and belonging.
Caregiver Strain: Caregivers, often grandparents or other relatives, may face significant challenges in providing for the child's needs, both emotionally and financially.

Financial Burdens

Families shoulder a significant financial burden due to incarceration, with some estimates suggesting families spend an average of $4,200 annually per incarcerated relative. In total, families incur nearly $350 billion in annual costs tied to incarceration.

Lost Income: Incarceration leads to a decline in family income for one in five families, with an average loss of $1,803 per month.
Court-Related Costs: Families often bear the brunt of court-related fines and fees, with 63% of families being primarily responsible for these costs.
Inability to Meet Basic Needs: 65% of families struggle to meet basic needs like food and housing due to the financial strain of incarceration.
Long-Term Economic Impact: Incarceration can negatively affect the incarcerated person's future earnings and job prospects, impacting the family's long-term financial stability.
Debt: Many families incur debt to pay for phone calls and visits to maintain contact with incarcerated family members.
Child Support: Incarceration can lead to difficulties in meeting child support obligations, and some recommend debt restructuring and suspension programs.

Contact with Children

Many incarcerated parents struggle to maintain contact with their children, with factors like distance, reluctance from the child's other parent, and the cost of visits contributing to reduced contact. Having contact between the incarcerated parent and their children is highly important for both the parent and the children. It can mitigate the negative impacts of parental incarceration, reduce the likelihood of future criminal behavior by the parent, and promote positive outcomes for the children.

Improved Well-being: Maintaining contact with an incarcerated parent can help children cope with the trauma of separation, reduce feelings of grief and loss, and provide a sense of stability. For the parent it can help improve in-prison behavior.
Stronger Family Ties: Contact can help maintain or strengthen family bonds, which can be a protective factor against future involvement in the criminal justice system.

Support Services

Providing access to mental health services, educational support, and family support programs is crucial for children of incarcerated parents.

  • Mental Health Services: Counseling and therapy can help children cope with trauma, grief, and emotional challenges.
  • Educational Support: Programs that provide tutoring, mentoring, and educational resources can help children stay on track academically.
  • Family Support Programs: These programs can provide financial assistance, parenting support, and help with basic needs.

Community Involvement

Communities can play a significant role in supporting these children.

  • Mentoring Programs: Connecting children with positive role models can provide guidance and support.
  • After-School Programs: These programs offer a safe environment, homework help, and extracurricular activities.
  • Community Awareness: Raising awareness about the challenges faced by these children can reduce stigma and encourage community support.

Collaboration

Collaboration among schools, social services, and the justice system can ensure comprehensive support for children.

  • Schools: Educators can be trained to understand and support children of incarcerated parents.
  • Social Services: Agencies can provide targeted assistance to these families.
  • Justice System: Policies can be implemented to maintain family connections and support rehabilitation.

Early Intervention

Early intervention is key to preventing negative outcomes for these children.

  • Early Childhood Programs: Programs like Head Start can provide a strong foundation for young children.
  • Parental Support: Providing support to incarcerated parents, such as parenting classes, can help them maintain a positive role in their children's lives.
  • Policy Changes: Advocating for policies that support family unity and reduce the impact of incarceration on children is essential.

MY PERSONAL FAVORITE RESOURCE

United Way 211

This is one of the best resources for families and children of incarcerated parents. It is a free and confidential referral and information helpline and website that connects people of all ages and from all communities to essential health and human services, 24 hours a day, seven days a week. The service is available in more than 180 languages, providing assistance to millions of people each year. By dialing 211, individuals can find help with a wide range of needs, including:

  • Basic Needs: Food, clothing, shelter, and financial assistance.
  • Health Resources: Medical, dental, and mental health services.
  • Employment Support: Job training, employment search assistance, and education programs.
  • Support for Older Adults and Persons with Disabilities: Home care, transportation, and other essential services.
  • Support for Children, Youth, and Families: Childcare, after-school programs, family counseling, and more.

United Way 211 is especially valuable for families of incarcerated parents because it helps them quickly connect to the support systems they need, reducing stress and improving stability during difficult times. Families can find local programs that offer everything from emotional support to practical resources for daily living. Visit United Way 211 Here
